Frequency & Regulatory

Part 15 ISM — not Part 97 Amateur Radio. MeshCore nodes in this network operate under FCC Part 15 (unlicensed) in the 902–928 MHz ISM band, centered at 915 MHz. The Part 15 power ceiling for spread-spectrum devices in this band is 30 dBm (1 W) conducted power with 6 dBi antenna, or equivalently 36 dBm EIRP. Licensed amateur operators may operate under Part 97 on nearby frequencies with higher power, but all Austin Mesh MeshCore nodes documented here are Part 15.

Antenna Notes (Managed Sites)

All managed-site antennas are omnidirectional verticals optimized for 902–928 MHz. The j-pole at Independent Condos provides a radiation pattern with minimal high-angle radiation (low takeoff angle) — appropriate for long-distance ground-level paths from that elevation. The 6 dBi fiberglass at Spring Condos provides ~6 dBi gain over an isotropic radiator (approximately 3.85 dBd over a half-wave dipole). At these heights, antenna gain selection matters less than site elevation and LOS to the surrounding terrain.

MeshCore vs. Meshtastic

Austin Mesh supports both. MeshCore is the primary focus for permanent metro infrastructure because repeater-centric routing suits hill-country terrain — the Barton Creek Escarpment and other features create RF shadows that reward high-elevation dedicated relay points over general-purpose flooding. Source: austinmesh.org.

MeshCore and Meshtastic are not inter-operable. They use different over-the-air protocols on the same 915 MHz band. A MeshCore repeater will not relay Meshtastic packets and vice versa. AISD school nodes (Meshtastic) are community partners but operate as a separate logical network.

Repeater Boards — Ranked by Community Preference

1. Ikoka (strongly preferred)

Most-recommended repeater board in the Austin community (269 Discord mentions). Peak TX current draw ~900 mA — lower than RAK 1W, which matters for solar and battery-backed systems. Output power ~30 dBm (1 W). Used at UT PMA (with cavity filter) and Auto Specialists repeater. "For a repeater build I'd take an Ikoka over these 9 times out of 10" — bplein.
Buy: ikoka.com

2. RAK 1W (RAK19007 base board + RAK4631 LoRa module)

Second-most discussed (85 mentions); ~30 dBm TX. Supports Serial interface for remote CLI access — useful for headless repeater management without physical access. Compatible with Waveshare Solar Power Manager D for solar builds.

Cost: ~$39 list, but ~$91/board after international shipping + import duties from RAK direct. Rokland (US distributor) is moderately cheaper.
FCC note (Mar 2026): Rokland received notice that RAK shipped boards without FCC approval; Rokland paused sales. Verify current compliance status before ordering — operating a non-FCC-authorized transmitter under Part 15 creates regulatory risk even for unlicensed operation.

Buy: Rokland (preferred US), RAK Wireless (international)

3. Heltec (older generation; usable with caveats)

136 Discord mentions; widely deployed but now considered older generation. Heltec T096 outputs ~700 mW (~28.5 dBm) — approximately 1.5 dB below the RAK/ikoka 1 W class. Requires a cavity filter (bandpass filter) to suppress out-of-band TX emissions and reduce receiver front-end desensitization from co-site or nearby interference sources. Tommy distributed cavity filters at an Austin Mesh meetup. Heltec V3 consistently noted for shorter range than RAK/ikoka class. Suitable for learning/testing; not recommended for new permanent infrastructure.

4. E22 Modules (EBYTE E22-900M30S / 900M33S)

45 Discord mentions; 30 dBm output. Used in Zebra Duo Pro and Station G2 hardware. Critical power note: The E22 module contains a fixed-gain power amplifier stage after the SX1262. MeshCore controls only the SX1262 drive level — setting it too high over-drives the PA and can permanently damage the E22. Community enforces an 8 dB drive limit. brad_98184: "MeshCore can only change the power level of the SX1262; the E22 module includes a power amp with a fixed amount of gain."

DIY Solar Repeater — Austin Mesh Lessons Learned

From austinmesh.org/devices/ — 5 hardware iterations over multiple Austin seasons (100°F+ summers, humid storms, hail). Current working version (v5): 12W solar panel → Voltaic Systems battery (USB output) → RAK board (USB input). Voltaic handles charge regulation and over-discharge protection with auto-reboot after recharge.

  1. Decouple the LoRa board from the solar/battery voltage. Feeding variable solar voltage directly into the RAK board causes it to enter "Super Deep Sleep" fault on low voltage — a state requiring physical reset. Use a Voltaic Systems battery as charge controller and buffered 5 V output.
  2. Solar panel: 10 W minimum. 0.4 W and 5 W panels both failed within one month under Austin summer insolation — insufficient to maintain battery state of charge through cloudy days.
  3. Battery: 5,000 mAh minimum. Voltaic Systems units include integrated solar charge controller and over-discharge cutoff — they auto-reboot after recharging, eliminating the need for a watchdog circuit.
  4. Antenna: fiberglass over rubber duck. Fiberglass handles UV degradation, wind, and rain over months/years. Rubber duck antennas degrade quickly outdoors; performance and pattern can shift as the dielectric ages.
  5. Shade the battery enclosure. Direct sunlight on the battery box causes thermal stress that accelerates capacity loss and shortens service life. Even a small solar shade over the battery section helps significantly.
  6. Omit GPS. A GPS module adds continuous RX current draw — significant over a season. Fixed repeaters don't need GPS; coordinates are set once in firmware.
  7. Weatherproofing is critical. Use O-rings on the antenna feedthrough, Proxicast tape on coax connectors, and a drain plug in the enclosure bottom. Apply conformal coating to the PCB in humid environments.
Power connection note: The JST connector on the solar board is the correct battery input — not USB. USB power is limited to ~1 A at 5 V (5 W), which is marginal for 30 dBm TX burst current. Always connect a battery to the JST port for full-power repeater operation. bplein bench test: RAK 1W ran 1+ hour on a 15 F supercapacitor alone with repeat disabled — illustrates how intermittent the TX duty cycle is at MeshCore advert intervals.

Multi-Byte Routing Path Hash (Recommended: 2-byte)

MeshCore identifies repeaters in routing tables using a hash of the device's public key. The default 1-byte hash yields only 254 unique IDs — collisions become frequent as the Austin network grows. Upgrading to 2-byte hashes gives 65,534 unique slots. Requires firmware ≥1.14.1.

set path.hash.mode 1   # 2-byte hash (recommended)
set path.hash.mode 2   # 3-byte hash (very large networks)
get path.hash.mode     # query current setting

After changing: reboot and issue a flood advertisement. MeshMapper auto-detects the byte mode from advert packets and updates the routing map automatically — no manual portal update required.

AUS region status (Jun 2026): 150 nodes total — 50 on 1-byte (33%), 92 on 2-byte (61%), 8 on 3-byte (5%). The 8 three-byte nodes need migration to 2-byte: BCW RAK 1W, EA 1W Solar, BCW RAK 4631, Solder Blob Repeater, CapRock RAK 1W, Buda Observer, CapRock SolarXiao, CapRock Solar Ikoka.

Channel Alignment

Austin Mesh nodes typically operate on channel 14, 15, or 16 (firmware channel numbers map to specific frequency offsets within the 902–928 MHz band). A companion that cannot hear its repeater is almost always a channel mismatch, not an RF path issue. Verify matching channels on the companion/repeater pair before troubleshooting any perceived coverage problem.

Flood Advertisement Interval

New repeaters should set a short flood advert interval (~1–2 hours) during initial deployment to establish routing table entries at neighboring nodes and appear in MeshMapper. After the node is well-established, lengthen the interval to reduce unnecessary channel occupancy.

Routing Table Entries vs. Direct Radio Contact

Routing table entries are not confirmed radio contacts. MeshMapper renders paths from node routing tables — which propagate through the mesh via flooding. A path entry "A → B" means A's firmware has heard that B exists at some point via some sequence of hops; it does not necessarily mean A and B have direct radio contact.

MeshMapper has no way to distinguish a routing entry established by direct LoRa reception from one that arrived as a routing advertisement relayed through 10 intermediate nodes — or one that arrived via an MQTT-bridged path from across the state.

Practical consequence: Many "paths" shown on the map for distant or terrain-obstructed pairs are propagated routing table entries, not direct RF links. The interactive RF map marks likely MQTT-bridged paths (distance >100 km) as dashed lines with a ⚠ indicator, and flags high Fresnel deficit paths (>50m deficit) as BLOCKED — geometrically incompatible with direct LoRa contact at this terrain and distance.

Duplicate Routing IDs

1-byte IDs = 254 possible combinations → collisions occur when two devices share a routing ID. MeshMapper flags colliders with red icons and excludes them from leaderboards. Resolution: affected operators upgrade to 2-byte hash mode (firmware ≥1.14.1). MeshMapper Hybrid wardriving mode auto-detects collisions using Discovery packets.

Free-Space Path Loss (FSPL)

FSPL represents the ideal signal attenuation between isotropic antennas in free space — no terrain, no atmosphere, no obstructions. It is the theoretical minimum path loss at a given frequency and distance:

FSPL (dB) = 32.44 + 20·log₁₀(f_MHz) + 20·log₁₀(d_km)
           = 32.44 + 20·log₁₀(915) + 20·log₁₀(d_km)
           = 91.67 + 20·log₁₀(d_km)

Received signal level (RSL) = EIRP − FSPL + RX antenna gain − other losses (coax, connectors, polarization mismatch, multipath). For an RSL above the receiver sensitivity threshold, the link closes. The margin above the noise floor determines robustness to fading and interference.

Why LoRa closes links that traditional narrowband systems cannot: SF12 LoRa has a noise floor approximately 125 dB below a 1 mW reference (+22 dBm EIRP - 125 dB noise floor ≈ −103 dBm noise floor relative, before spreading gain). The 40 dB margin above FSPL at 13 km means a link can tolerate 40 dB of additional attenuation from diffraction, knife-edge obstacles, foliage, or multipath before falling below the receiver sensitivity floor — far more than the few dB of margin typical in conventional narrowband land-mobile links.

Fresnel Zone Analysis

RF path clearance analysis for the Austin MeshCore network using USGS 3DEP terrain data. Quantifies clearance of the first Fresnel zone for every routing table entry with confirmed endpoints. See the interactive map for full per-path data with hover tooltips and antenna height sensitivity tables.

What Is the First Fresnel Zone?

The first Fresnel zone (F1) is an ellipsoid around the direct path between two antennas. Energy traveling through the zone arrives at the receiver in phase — obstruction of this zone causes destructive interference that attenuates the received signal even when a geometric LOS exists. The industry rule for point-to-point links is to maintain 60% of the F1 radius as clearance above terrain. The F1 radius at the midpoint of a path is:

r₁ (m) = 17.3 · √(d_km / (4 · f_GHz))    (midpoint, approximate)
        = 17.3 · √(d_km / (4 · 0.915))
        ≈ 9.04 · √d_km   (at 915 MHz)

At 13 km (Oak Hill → Independent Condos): F1 midpoint radius ≈ 32.6 m. 60% clearance requires ≥ 19.5 m above terrain at the midpoint.

Path Status Definitions

StatusMap ColorRF MeaningLoRa Practical Impact
CLEAR■ Green≥60% F1 Fresnel clearance; full LOSFull link quality; no diffraction penalty
MARGINAL■ YellowClearance just above 60% F1; <5 m marginMinor sensitivity to vegetation growth, multipath, or wet-foliage conditions
OBSTRUCTED■ RedSub-F1 clearance; NLOS via terrain diffractionSignal propagates via knife-edge or rounded-hill diffraction. With ~40 dB budget margin above FSPL, most OBSTRUCTED paths are functional. Typical for urban/suburban LoRa.
BLOCKED■ PurpleFresnel deficit >50 m; geometrically incompatible with direct LoRa contact at this frequency, distance, and terrainAlmost certainly a routing table entry propagated via intermediate hops — not a direct radio contact between these two nodes. Treat as routing topology data, not RF link data.
OBSTRUCTED ≠ non-functional. The 60% Fresnel rule was developed for traditional microwave point-to-point links where a few dB of margin is all that separates usable from unusable. LoRa SF12 has roughly 40 dB of budget margin above FSPL — enough to absorb substantial knife-edge diffraction losses while remaining above the −130 dBm receiver sensitivity floor. Expect most OBSTRUCTED paths to work in practice. BLOCKED paths with >50 m Fresnel deficit are geometrically incompatible with direct LoRa regardless of budget margin.

Understanding how 915 MHz signals propagate over Austin's terrain is essential for interpreting coverage maps and planning node placement.

Barton Creek Escarpment

The dominant RF barrier in the Austin metro. The escarpment rises from ~240 m MSL in Oak Hill to >370 m MSL at the limestone rim — an obstacle wall that blocks direct paths from southwest Austin (Oak Hill, Dripping Springs) to downtown. The tgh0831-repeater at 1.5 m AGL shows Fresnel deficits of 32–50 m to Digitaino (downtown) and UT1 — not correctable by any practical antenna height. Paths to downtown from Oak Hill route through the WVARC (Wimberley) → downtown chain, or via elevated relay nodes that clear the escarpment horizon.

Local Clutter vs. Terrain

The sensitivity sweep in each repeater popup (map) distinguishes local clutter from terrain obstruction. If adding 10 m of antenna height improves Fresnel margin by less than ~3 m (a gain slope <0.3 m per meter of height added), the obstruction is near-field clutter (trees, structures within 1–3 km) — not solvable by raising the antenna at the far end. If the gain slope is ≥1 m per meter, the obstruction is a terrain feature on the path that can be cleared with antenna height.

Governance & Trademark Drama

Background on a conflict between the open-source MeshCore firmware community and an external figure (AndyK / ripplebiz) who attempted to assert brand ownership over MeshCore.

Current state (Jun 2026): The MeshCore GitHub repository (meshcore-dev/MeshCore, MIT license) remains under core developer control — Liam (NZ) and Scott retained repo ownership. AndyK rebranded to "Ripple/MeshOS" and is largely absent from community channels. Open-source client tooling has improved: meshcore-open (Flutter) and MeshMapper Flutter app are both now open-source. Austin Mesh is not directly affected by the trademark issue, which is UK-jurisdiction only.
